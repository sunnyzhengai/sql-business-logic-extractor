## Definition

The reusable structure for weekly Saturday articles on the content site. Each article follows the same eight-section shape so readers develop a rhythm and so production can be semi-automated. Voice, tone, and constraints are governed by the TIP strategy (`decisions/2026-04-19-thinking-in-public-strategy.md`).

## The eight sections

### 1. Cold open (1–3 sentences)

Drop the reader into a scene or question. No setup, no throat-clearing, no "In today's article we will..." Voice established in the first sentence.

### 2. The pain (2–4 paragraphs)

Concrete, industry-agnostic scenario. Reader must recognize themselves in the first paragraph. Plain language; no jargon until earned. This is where the public version of internal healthcare scenes (Patient A01, silent upstream break) gets translated into Orders/Customers/Invoices generic form.

### 3. Two positions, fairly stated (~1 paragraph each)

"One camp says X, because..."   "Another camp says Y, because..." Both must sound defensible. Straw-manning either costs credibility. If the reader is in camp X, they should feel heard in that paragraph; same for camp Y.

### 4. Where I've landed (1–3 paragraphs)

One of three modes:

- **(a) Clear stance with reasons from experience.** Used when the answer is genuinely defensible and field-tested.
- **(b) Neither is fully right — here's the third path.** The signature move. Used for forks where the site has an opinionated non-obvious position (catch-up vs. spec-first is the canonical example).
- **(c) Honestly unsure — here's what I'd need to see.** Used sparingly, only for genuinely open forks. Don't fake uncertainty when you have a view.

Voice convention: "I've spent years watching this play out." NOT "As a [role] at [employer]." Credentials-light, experience-heavy. TIP rule.

### 5. What a solution would need (2–3 paragraphs)

Theoretical requirements, not products. "A tool that could do X would change this." NEVER "I'm building X." This is the stealth requirements document — readers who need the capability recognize themselves as prospects without ever being sold to.

### 6. The vote (1 paragraph + poll)

Direct question to the reader. Standard format: A / B / C (and sometimes D) + "I've never thought about this as a choice."

The last option is not a throwaway — it captures readers who haven't yet framed the question, which is often the largest segment. Collecting that segment accurately is as valuable as collecting committed-A or committed-B readers.

### 7. Call for stories (1 sentence)

"Tell me about the version of this you've lived through." Drives comments, DMs, and email replies. Under TIP, this is the pipeline. Readers who reply are self-selecting into your prospect list.

### 8. Next-week hook (optional, 1 sentence)

Used only when articles build on each other. Skip when the article is standalone.

## Tone rules

- **Length:** 700–1200 words. Shorter is better if the idea is sharp.
- **Paragraphs:** 3–4 sentences max. One idea per paragraph.
- **Examples:** concrete before abstract, specific before general.
- **Industry-agnostic:** Orders, Customers, Invoices, Subscriptions — never Patient, Encounter, HEDIS, Epic.
- **Problem / solution ratio:** 90/10. Public content exists to establish the market gap, not to showcase the solution.
- **No product names in public content.** No AIVIA, no "our engine," no "the tool I'm building." "Exploring" posture throughout.
- **Pre-prepared stealth reply** for readers who ask if you have a tool. See strategy decision page.

## What the template is NOT

- Not a rigid script — the section boundaries can blur, sentence counts can shift. The structure is a skeleton for consistency, not a straitjacket.
- Not a substitute for a good idea. A weak fork stays weak even when templated. The wiki's job is to keep the idea supply high; this template is for turning ideas into publishable pieces.
- Not the only valid shape. Occasional off-format pieces (pure scene, pure analysis, guest commentary) are fine if the cumulative corpus has a recognizable rhythm.

## Open questions

- Is the 700–1200 word range right, or should the cadence favor shorter pieces (400–600) for social-sharing velocity? TIP suggests medium-length pieces perform best on LinkedIn for thought-leadership positioning — verify once publishing begins.
- Should poll results be surfaced live (reader sees aggregate immediately after voting) or weekly (following Saturday's article references last week's outcome)? Live aggregate is more engaging; weekly reveal creates return-visit hook.
- How to handle articles where none of the three "where I've landed" modes fit cleanly? (e.g., "here's three stances, pick yours" genuinely open forks.) Probably a fourth mode, used rarely.
